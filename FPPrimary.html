Kindergarten

The Kindergarten is for children from the age of around three years old until they start grade school at six or seven years old. It is comparable to preschool in America. Attending a Kindergarten is not mandatory in Germany and, different from the rest of German schooling, it is not free. Children whose parents want to put them in Kindergarten have a legal right to get a spot and the government helps low income parents afford the fees. Despite shortages of spots, a majority of children attend Kindergarten before they start grade school. Kindergartens can be run by the city or by independent organizations like churches, companies or associations. They can operate under different concepts such as Montessori, Waldorf, forest- or bilingual Kindergartens, but the main focus of Kindergartens is typically to improve children’s social, motor and emotional skills and let them play (1). When I was in Kindergarten, we spent our days playing with blocks, dolls or costumes, drawing, playing in the sandbox or being read to and we would go play in the forest once a week, no matter what the weather was like. In the last year of Kindergarten, we spent some time separately from the other children to learn colors and how to write our name.

Grundschule

Children typically start grade school (Grundschule) at the age of six. Grade school lasts four years and is mandatory for all children. Homeschooling is not an option in Germany. In typical German grade schools, the school day is very short. School lasts from 8am to somewhere between 11.15am and 1pm depending on the age of the students. In the first two years of grade school students don’t receive grades and the report cards consist of a descriptive evaluation of a student’s skills and behavior. In third and fourth grade students are graded on a scale of 1-6. In grade school students learn the foundations of reading, writing and math and typically have classes in arts and crafts, physical education, English, science and local geography, religion or ethics and music. Fourth grade, the last year of Grundschule in Germany is very important to students’ futures. At the end of fourth grade students, who are typically around ten years old, are sorted into one of three kinds of secondary schools based on their grades in the subjects German, Math and Science/Local Geography and their teacher’s recommendation. In my experience the pressure to get good grades in fourth grade was immense for most students. We usually had homework that would take all afternoon to finish. Many students’ parents got them tutors to help them perform in school. A common thing I heard was that you had to get into the higher-level school so that you would be able to get a good job in the future. Students whose grades don’t clearly fall into one of the three school choices have the option to take a three-day test class which is graded.

Grades required for the transfer to a secondary school

The grades in Germany are on a scale from 1 - 6, with 1 being the best. The grade average required to attend the different secondary schools varies from state to state. In Bavaria students need to get a 2.33 (Which is approximately equivalent to a US B-) or better to attend the Gymnasium and a 2.66 (approximately a US C+) to attend the Realschule. School times

German school times vary depending on the ages of the students. Most schools start at 8:00. Gradeschoolers start out finishing school at 11:30 in the first year and then slowly increase the time to 12:15 or 1pm on a couple days per week. In the different secondary schools school typically ends at 1:00 on most days with older students having classes until 15:15 or (rarely) 17:00 on a couple of days.
